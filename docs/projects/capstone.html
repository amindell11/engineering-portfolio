Reactive Robot Arm
2025–2026
ROS 2 & MoveIt

Overview

I built an autonomous ball-catching robotic arm that detects a thrown ball, predicts its trajectory, and moves to intercept — all within a sub-second window of flight. I designed and integrated the full sensing-to-actuation pipeline: an xArm5 robot arm, a Time-of-Flight depth camera for 3D workspace mapping, a FLIR machine-vision tracking camera, and a ROS 2 / MoveIt control stack that plans collision-free motions in real time.

Physical lab setup — xArm5 with overhead ToF depth camera and catch workspace
System architecture — ToF obstacle detection, ball tracking camera, intercept decider, and motion controller

Perception & Ball Detection

I developed a multi-sensor perception pipeline that delivers reliable 3D ball tracking at camera frame rate:

  • Time-of-Flight depth camera: Mounted overhead to generate a dense point cloud of the workspace. I used this for environment mapping, obstacle detection, and sensor-to-robot extrinsic calibration.
  • Tracking camera: Implemented color-based segmentation on a Kowa 3.5 mm wide-angle lens paired with a FLIR machine-vision camera, producing real-time 3D ball position estimates each frame.
  • Ball state estimation: Designed a ballistic state estimator that fits a physics-based projectile model to just a few frames of detected positions, predicting the full trajectory within milliseconds of release.
Foxglove visualization — ToF point cloud with live camera feed of the xArm5 in the lab
Real-time ball detection — color segmentation with bounding box tracking

Trajectory Prediction & Workspace

I computed the xArm5's reachable workspace envelope from its kinematic model and used it as a real-time gate: the system only commits to a catch when the predicted trajectory intersects the catchable zone. This eliminated wasted motion on unreachable throws and let the controller focus planning time on viable intercepts.

Ball state estimator output — color-coded trajectory predictions with obstacle positions

Motion Planning & Control

I implemented a Model Predictive Controller (MPC) backed by a QP solver that generates real-time joint velocity commands. The controller optimizes a cost function subject to joint limits and obstacle constraints, producing smooth, collision-free motions fast enough to reach the intercept point before the ball arrives.

Testing & Results

I validated the full pipeline from Gazebo simulation through closed-loop catch attempts with live ball throws. Verified ball-tracking accuracy and joint-position tracking in real time using Foxglove, iterating on estimator tuning and controller gains to close the gap between simulated and physical performance.
