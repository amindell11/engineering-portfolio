OSV Autonomous Vehicle

ENES100 — "Urban Turtle" Over-Sand Vehicle

University of Maryland
SolidWorks & Arduino

Overview

The "Urban Turtle" was an autonomous Over-Sand Vehicle (OSV) built for the University of Maryland's ENES100 course. Teams were challenged to engineer a vehicle capable of navigating a sandy terrain course autonomously — traversing obstacles, identifying ArUco markers, and reaching designated waypoints without human intervention. The project spanned the full engineering cycle: conceptual design, CAD modeling in SolidWorks, prototyping, and iterative testing on a physical sand course.

Chassis Structure

The chassis was built around a thick UHMW (ultra-high-molecular-weight polyethylene) base-plate chosen for its combination of strength, light weight, and machinability. The structure was designed for easy maintenance and modularity:

  • Mounting slots for chain tensioning, allowing drivetrain adjustments without disassembly.
  • Access hole for battery replacement from below without removing the electronics deck.
  • Threaded inserts embedded in the base-plate to mount 3D-printed sensor brackets and electronics enclosures.
  • Protective cover for the electronics, shielding the Arduino and wiring from sand and debris.
  • Two-tier standoff architecture separating the battery compartment (lower) from the controller deck (upper), with cross-section profiles optimized for a low center of gravity.

Overall footprint was approximately 270 × 280 mm with a height of 121 mm (excluding tires), kept compact to satisfy the course size constraints.

Propulsion System

The Urban Turtle used a chain-driven four-wheel-drive system powered by DC gear motors. Each motor drove a sprocket via roller chain, transferring torque to large off-road tires selected for maximum traction on loose sand. Key specifications:

  • Motors: DC gear motors with a no-load speed of ~265 RPM and a stall torque of ~25 oz-in, providing sufficient low-end torque for sand traversal.
  • Chain drive: Roller chain and sprocket transmission chosen over direct drive for its mechanical advantage and ability to absorb shock loads from terrain irregularities.
  • Tires: Oversized rubber tires with aggressive tread pattern, maximizing ground contact area and preventing the vehicle from sinking into soft sand.
  • Skid-steer turning: Differential speed between left and right motor pairs enabled zero-radius turns for navigating tight spaces around obstacles.

Autonomous Navigation

The vehicle navigated the sand course autonomously using an Arduino microcontroller coordinating multiple sensor inputs and a WiFi communication module. The navigation strategy involved:

  • ArUco marker localization: An overhead vision system tracked the vehicle's position on the course via an ArUco marker mounted on a vertical mast, providing real-time x/y coordinates and heading angle to the Arduino over WiFi.
  • Ultrasonic obstacle detection: Forward-facing ultrasonic sensors detected rocks and terrain features, triggering avoidance maneuvers when obstacles fell within a threshold distance.
  • Waypoint sequencing: The control algorithm drove the vehicle through a series of GPS-like waypoints, computing heading error at each step and issuing proportional steering corrections via differential motor speeds.
  • WiFi communication: A WiFi module maintained a live link to the course's vision system, receiving position updates and transmitting telemetry for real-time debugging.

Design Iteration

The Urban Turtle went through multiple design iterations, progressing from a proof-of-concept prototype to the final competition vehicle:

  • Prototype v0 (metal chassis): An early build using a perforated metal base-plate with small plastic wheels. This version validated the basic electronics and motor control but lacked the traction and ground clearance needed for sand.
  • Prototype v1 (wooden chassis): Upgraded to a laser-cut wooden base-plate with significantly larger off-road tires. This iteration improved sand performance dramatically but revealed chain alignment and tensioning issues.
  • Final design (UHMW chassis): The competition vehicle incorporated all lessons learned — UHMW base-plate for durability, adjustable chain tensioning slots, 3D-printed sensor mounts, and a protective electronics enclosure.
