Wildfire Spread Prediction

Deep Learning for Next-Day Wildfire Spread Forecasting — LMU Deep Learning Lab

Research
2024–Present
Vision Transformers & XAI

Overview

This research, conducted at the LMU Deep Learning Lab, applies deep learning to the problem of next-day wildfire spread prediction using multi-channel geospatial data. The work spans two phases: a completed study on explainable AI for wildfire modeling using Vision Transformers, and ongoing research reproducing and extending Swin Transformer-based temporal prediction methods.

The models take as input 12 geospatial feature channels — including elevation, wind direction, wind velocity, temperature, humidity, precipitation, drought index, vegetation density, population density, energy release component, and previous fire masks — and predict the next-day fire spread mask as a segmentation output.

Explainable AI for Wildfire Modeling (Completed)

The first phase of research focused on training and comparing multiple deep learning architectures for wildfire spread prediction, then applying explainable AI (XAI) techniques to understand which geospatial features drive model predictions. The models trained include:

  • Vision Transformer (ViT): Patch-based transformer architecture applied to multi-channel geospatial imagery for fire mask segmentation.
  • Baseline Autoencoder: Encoder-decoder architecture providing a baseline for comparison against transformer models.
  • ResNet: Deep residual network adapted for the geospatial segmentation task.
  • UNet: Encoder-decoder with skip connections, a standard architecture for image segmentation tasks.

To interpret model behavior, three XAI techniques were applied across all architectures:

  • SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations): Computed feature-level importance scores using both TreeSHAP (for XGBoost baselines) and DeepSHAP (for neural networks).
  • GradCAM: Generated class activation heatmaps highlighting spatial regions most influential in predictions.
  • Integrated Gradients: Attribution method quantifying per-feature contribution to the model output.

The original paper can be found on arXiv.

Data Collection & Fire Mapping

Geospatial data was collected using ArcGIS, Google Earth Engine API, and NASA VIIRS satellite fire detection products. Fire perimeters were mapped and overlaid on satellite imagery to build the training dataset.

Model Architectures

Multiple deep learning architectures were implemented and compared for the fire spread segmentation task. The Swin-UNet adapts a Swin Transformer encoder-decoder with shifted window attention for efficient spatial modeling, while the UNet provides a convolutional baseline with skip connections.

Swin Transformer Temporal Prediction (Ongoing)

The second phase of research focuses on reproducing and extending results from Lahrichi et al. (2025), which adapts the SwinUnet architecture — originally developed for medical image segmentation — to wildfire spread prediction. This work uses the WildfireSpreadTS (WFTS) benchmark dataset containing 607 wildfire events across the western United States (2018–2021), totaling over 13,000 daily multi-channel images across 23 channels at 375m resolution.

Key aspects of this ongoing work include:

  • Single-day vs. multi-day input: Comparing model performance when given a single snapshot versus a 5-day temporal sequence of geospatial conditions.
  • ImageNet pre-training: Evaluating the benefit of transfer learning from pre-trained Swin-Tiny weights versus training from scratch on the wildfire domain.
  • 12-fold cross-validation: Leave-one-year-out evaluation strategy across the full WFTS dataset for robust performance estimation.
  • Average Precision (AP): Primary evaluation metric for comparing against prior convolutional and attention-based baselines (Res18-Unet, Res50-Unet, UTAE).
